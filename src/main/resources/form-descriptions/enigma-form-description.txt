The Enigma machine is a cipher device that was developed in the early 20th century and was used extensively by Nazi Germany during World War II. The Enigma machine consists of several key components: a keyboard (for entering letters), a plugboard (which swaps pairs of letters before and after rotor encryption), three rotors with notched rings (which dynamically scramble letters and change the substitution after each keypress), and a reflector (which redirects the electrical signal back through the rotors, enabling encryption and decryption with the same machine settings). The Enigma cipher was first broken by Polish cryptanalysts in the early 1930s. Their work was eventually picked up by France and Britain in 1939, after which Alan Turing and other codebreakers expanded the shared knowledge and broke the German Enigma communications during World War II.
