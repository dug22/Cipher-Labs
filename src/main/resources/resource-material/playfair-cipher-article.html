Details Behind the Playfair Cipher

What is the Playfair Cipher?

The Playfair cipher is a classical digraph substitution cipher that was created by Charles Wheatstone in 1854. The cipher was named after its advertiser, Lord Playfair. It's called a digraph substitution cipher due to its nature of encrypting pairs of letters, instead of a single alphabet. The cipher was used during the Boer War, World War I, and World War II.

The Encryption Process

Creating a Key

The key can be composed of random letters or the key can be a short or long word, which uses any 25-letter (I/J are combined) combination that doesn't recur.

5X5 Polybius Square

Next, construct a 5x5 grid, and each square must contain letters of your key first, along with the rest of the alphabet. Note each letter can only be used once, and what I mean by that is, say if your key was "CAB", for the first three squares (by row), you'd write "C", "A", "B", then continue to fill the rest of the squares with letters that weren't used from the alphabet, "D", "E", etc. Remember I/J share one square. Splitting Your Plain Text into Pairs

The encryption process for Playfair's cipher requires you to divide your message into letter pairs. If any pairs contain the same letter, it is necessary to insert the filler character ‘X’ between the duplicate letters (or ‘Z’ if the duplicate pair is ‘XX’). If your text ends up having an odd length, just insert the filler letter 'X' (or 'Z' to avoid a duplicate pair of 'XX') to make the length even. If your message, for example, was "HELLO WORLD", you'd break the text up like this: "HE LX LO WO RL DX". Here's another example: if your plaintext message was "FOX XRAY", you'd break the text up like this: "FO XZ XR AY". After breaking up your text, you'd use the 5x5 grid to encrypt each letter pair.

Next, split the plaintext message into letter pairs. The plaintext message "HELLO WORLD" becomes "HE LX LO WO RL DX". Each letter pair is encrypted using the grid based on one of three specific rules:

  1. Rule 1:If both letters of the letter pair share the same column, take the letter beneath it (redirect back to the top if the letter of the letter pair is at the bottom.)
  2. Rule 2:If both letters of the letter pair share the same row, take the most right letter (redirect back to the leftmost if at the rightmost position).
  3. Rule 3:If neither rules 1 nor 2 are applicable, form a rectangle with the two letters within the grid, and use the letter on the horizontal opposite of the rectangle.

Security Implications

The Playfair cipher is a unique cipher on its own, but it is highly vulnerable to hill climbing attacks, and frequency analysis, especially digraph attacks, if there is enough text to go around. Playfair is considered better suited to short messages than to long ones.
